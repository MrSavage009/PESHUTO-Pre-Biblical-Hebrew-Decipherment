## Abstract

Traditional translations of the Hebrew Bible are frequently treated as neutral linguistic records. In historical-critical philology, however, they are recognized as highly curated theological interpretations. Over centuries, various scribal traditions (*soferim*) and later ecclesiastical translators systematically overlaid primordial Semitic vocabularies with highly centralized, vertical, and monarchical concepts—a process of semantic occultation. 

This paper introduces the **PESHUTO (פְּשׁוּטוֹ) Framework**, an AI-engineered revisionist semantic engine designed to reverse-engineer these theological overlays. Named after the classical Hebrew term for the literal, unadorned, contextual sense of a text (*Peshuto shel Mikra*), the framework strips away later theological biases. 

By integrating a browser-based, hardware-agnostic runtime with a large language model (Gemini), *Peshuto* executes a dual-methodology analysis on target words: **Comparative Semitic Cognate Reconstruction** (Methodology I - Orange Axis) and **Contextual Gap-Fill Cloze Analysis** (Methodology II - Purple Axis) across a restricted seven-verse context window.

## 1. Introduction & Epistemological Stance

Every translation is an act of historical curation. In the context of ancient Near Eastern literature, the transition from decentralized, West-Semitic clan structures to centralized Israelite monarchies, and eventually to second-temple and Rabbinic Judaism, caused a profound shift in language. Pre-biblical Northwest Semitic dialects were deeply rooted in horizontal social contracts, kinship obligations, and ecological realities. However, later theological translators systematically verticalized these terms. 

Horizontal pacts became vertical "mercy"; familial or tribal compacts became feudal "goodness" or divine "grace" dictated by a supreme monarch.

The **PESHUTO Framework** operates on a critical revisionist premise: *the traditional scribal translations of the biblical corpus contain systemic theological bias designed to obscure earlier, horizontal, socio-contractual meanings.* 

To expose and correct this bias, *Peshuto* bypasses standard lexicons (which are themselves heavily reliant on the medieval and Masoretic traditions) and uses advanced LLM prompting architectures to analyze the text. It evaluates target words from two distinct angles: etymological tracking of roots across non-biblical ancient Semitic cognate systems, and raw contextual syntax analysis using a target-omitted (cloze) methodology.

### Layer 0 (L0): The Primordial Archive
All raw textual data, comparative cognate records, and seven-verse context windows are ingested into an immutable, content-addressed database. By generating SHA-256 keys for each text block, the engine prevents arbitrary alteration of source materials during live execution.

### Layer 1 (L1): Local Manifold Space
L1 maps local semantic transitions. Instead of treating vocabulary as static dictionary definitions, meaning is modeled as a geodesic distance across a weighted graph of conceptual nodes. Distance parameters dynamically adjust based on context.

### Layer 2 (L2): The Revisionist Prompt Gateway
L2 is the active processing engine. When triggered, it collects the immutable L0 context window, packages it within a structured JSON prompt template, and sends it directly to the Gemini API endpoint. This layer manages the two primary revisionist methodologies.

### Layer 3 (L3): The Verifiable Commit Ledger
To ensure all interpretive experiments are auditable, every successful API execution and its resulting semantic matrix are appended to a local, tamper-evident Merkle Tree. The calculated Merkle Root provides a permanent cryptographic receipt of the analysis.

## 3. Core Methodologies

The engine splits its analysis into two distinct methodologies to verify its findings.

```
                  [ 7-Verse Context Window ]
                              │
               ┌──────────────┴──────────────┐
               ▼                             ▼
     [ Methodology I ]             [ Methodology II ]
       (Orange Axis)                 (Purple Axis)
     Comparative Semitic           Contextual Cloze
    Cognate Reconstruction        Gap-Fill Analysis
               │                             │
               ▼                             ▼
       Raw Root meaning             10 Possible Meanings
      from Non-Biblical            reconstructed from the
     historical sources            structural environment
```

### 3.1. Methodology I: Comparative Semitic Cognate Reconstruction (The Orange Axis)

This methodology bypasses later biblical theological developments. It targets the etymological root of a word using comparative Semitic linguistics, drawing on non-biblical, epigraphic, and historical sources:

$$\text{Root}_{\text{revised}} = f\left(\text{Cognates}_{\text{Ugaritic}}, \text{Cognates}_{\text{Akkadian}}, \text{Cognates}_{\text{Phoenician}}, \text{Cognates}_{\text{Arabic}}\right)$$

By prioritizing ancient treaty texts, legal tablets, and administrative records over theological manuscripts, the AI reconstructs the word's earliest horizontal, socio-contractual meanings.

### 3.2. Methodology II: Contextual Gap-Fill Cloze Analysis (The Purple Axis)

To prevent etymological over-reliance, this methodology uses a purely structural approach. The engine takes the target verse, along with its immediate historical and literary environment:

$$\text{Context} = [V_{-3}, V{-2}, V_{-1}, V_{\text{target}}, V_{+1}, V_{+2}, V_{+3}]$$

The engine strips the target word from $V_{\text{target}}$, replacing it with a blank gap: `[_______]`. 

It then instructs the AI to evaluate the 7-verse window without any theological preconceptions, identifying which semantic concepts would logically fit the gap based on local syntax, narrative flow, and historical plausibility. This analysis produces exactly ten alternative terms, complete with contextual justifications.

## 4. Operational Case Study: Reconstructing *Hesed* (חֶסֶד)

To demonstrate the engine's capabilities, we analyze the highly central biblical term *Hesed* (חֶסֶד) in **Psalm 136:1**, typically translated as "mercy," "lovingkindness," or "steadfast love."

```

"Communal Boundary"         "Covenant Fealty" /

obligation (Ugaritic)       Based on treaty context
```

### 4.1. Historical Occultation
Scribal translations consistently render *hesed* as an act of vertical pity or divine grace extended by a sovereign to a subordinate. This interpretation reinforces monarchical hierarchy.

### 4.2. Methodology I Analysis (Orange Axis)
When analyzed using non-biblical cognate databases, the engine traces the root to the West-Semitic and Ugaritic root *h-s-d*, which refers to a mutual horizontal safeguard, clan protective obligation, or communal boundary compact. 

The revised meaning shifts from vertical "mercy" to a horizontal **"Communal Boundary Safeguard."**

### 4.3. Methodology II Analysis (Purple Axis)
Using the 7-verse cloze context window of Psalm 136, the target word is treated as a gap `[_______]`. Given the historical setting of the psalm—which outlines Israelite tribal confederacies, regional battles, and the acquisition of territory—the engine identifies that vertical "mercy" is structurally out of place. Instead, it generates a list of ten highly logical, horizontal semantic fit-ins:

1. **Covenant Fealty:** Reflects the rigid regional treaties of the ancient Near East.
2. **Alliance Shield:** Aligns with the military defense and land-acquisition context.
3. **Mutual Safeguard:** Upholds horizontal pacts made among early Levantine clans.
4. **Tribal Protectorate:** Frames the deity as an active military ally.
5. **Feudal Pact:** Matches the legal expectations of regional suzerain-vassal treaties.
